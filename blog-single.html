Apostille Guide

Navigating the Hague Convention: A Practical Guide

Oct 12, 2026 Robert Hayes

When dealing with international documents, the term "Apostille" frequently comes up. Established by the Hague Convention of 1961, an apostille is a specialized certificate that authenticates the origin of a public document so it can be recognized in foreign countries that are members of the convention.

1. Is the Destination Country a Member?

Before requesting an apostille, you must verify that the country where the document will be used is a member of the Hague Apostille Convention. If it is not, a more complex, multi-step legalization process through the country's embassy or consulate is required.

"The apostille simplifies the legalization of documents to a single formality: the issuance of an authentication certificate by an authority designated by the State."

2. Prepare the Original Document

Apostilles are typically affixed to original public documents or certified true copies. For instance, if you need to apostille a birth certificate, it must be a certified original from the state department of health, not a photocopy.

3. Notarization Pre-requisite

For many private documents, such as a Power of Attorney or corporate bylaws, the document must first be properly notarized by a commissioned notary public. The Secretary of State then authenticates the notary's signature and seal.
